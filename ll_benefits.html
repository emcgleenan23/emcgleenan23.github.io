Why heat + cold

The science of the sweat

Sauna bathing is one of the best-studied wellness habits there is. Here's what the research actually says.

Case study · Kirby et al., 2020

Three weeks of sauna, measurable gains

Twenty middle-distance runners (13 women, average age 20) added post-exercise sauna sessions of about half an hour at 101–108°C, a few times per week for 3 weeks — 12 sessions in total. Before and after, all athletes completed heat tolerance and fitness tests measuring core temperature, heart rate, and endurance performance. Compared with the control group, the sauna group showed:

Cooler under pressure

Lower core body temperature and heart rate during exercise in the heat.

Bigger engine

Improved aerobic capacity — VO₂max rose by 0.27 L/min.

Faster for free

Better running speed at a given intensity (+0.6 km/h).

The practical takeaway: adding sauna sessions after exercise for just 3 weeks can help the body adapt to heat and boost endurance.

Contrast therapy

Why the cold plunge matters

The magic isn't just the heat — it's the switch. Moving between a 80–90°C sauna and a cold plunge trains your circulation, sharpens your head, and delivers that unmistakable post-sauna calm. Our recommended 45-minute rhythm:

  1. Start: 1–2 min cold plunge
  2. 10–15 min in the sauna
  3. 1–2 min cold plunge
  4. 10–15 min in the sauna
  5. Finish: 1–2 min cold plunge

Good to know

Listen to your body

Stay hydrated, step out whenever you need to, and build up cold exposure gradually — a minute or two is plenty.

Sauna bathing is not a substitute for medical advice. If you have any medical condition that may be affected by heat, cold or extreme temperatures — or you're pregnant — seek advice from a medical professional before using our facilities. Source: Kirby et al., 2020.
